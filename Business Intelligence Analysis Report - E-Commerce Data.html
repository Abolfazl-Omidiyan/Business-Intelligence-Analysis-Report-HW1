🔍 Impact of Duplicates

  • Statistical Bias: Frequently repeated queries (e.g., "School bag" appearing 99 times) artificially inflate their statistical weight
  • Distribution Distortion: Mean, variance, and quantiles are pulled toward repeated values
  • Assumption Violation: Duplicates violate independence assumption required for most statistical tests
  • Model Impact: Machine learning models overfit on popular queries

⚠️ Impact of Missing Data

  • 14.3% of products lack price information – Cannot include in price-based analyses
  • MNAR Risk (Missing Not At Random): If expensive/budget items systematically lack prices, we get distorted market view
  • Reduced Statistical Power: Smaller effective sample size reduces ability to detect true effects
  • Solutions: Imputation (fill with category median), deletion (conservative), or specialized missing-data models

📈 Distribution Theory

This distribution is characteristic of e-commerce systems and follows a Power-law or Pareto distribution. The "80/20 rule" applies: ~80% of retrievals come from ~20% of products. Understanding this helps with inventory management and recommendation algorithms.

⚠️ Key Finding: Data is NOT Normally Distributed

  • Evidence: Points deviate strongly from red reference line, especially in tails
  • P-value < 0.0001: 99.99% confidence rejecting normality hypothesis
  • Heavy tails: More extreme values than normal distribution predicts
  • Implication: Power-law or Log-normal model is more appropriate

💡 Statistical Implications

  • Student's t-test invalid: Assumes normality; use at own risk
  • Alternative tests recommended:
    • Mann-Whitney U test (instead of t-test)
    • Kruskal-Wallis H test (instead of ANOVA)
    • Bootstrap methods for confidence intervals

Dominant Category & Chi-Square Test

Statistical Test of Category-Click Independence

Identify the dominant category for each query and test whether membership in the dominant category is statistically associated with user click behavior using Chi-Square test of independence.

⚠️ Finding: NO Significant Association

With p-value = 1.000 (>> 0.05), we FAIL to reject H₀. There is NO statistically significant association between membership in the dominant category and click behavior. Dominant category alone does not predict whether user will click.

💡 Why No Significant Relationship?

  • Limited data overlap: Product ID mismatch between query results and product DB reduces sample
  • User behavior complexity: Category dominance is weak signal; other factors dominate (rank, title, reviews)
  • Implication: Search ranking and presentation matter more than category diversity
